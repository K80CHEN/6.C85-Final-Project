Myth #1 Migrating is "easy"

The perception of how easy or difficult it is to migrate to the United States varies among Americans and is influenced by personal experience, political affiliation, and geographic location. While some believe that the current immigration system needs major changes, with 24% saying it should be completely rebuilt, according to a Pew Research Center survey conducted in 2019, others believe that it is relatively easy to migrate to the US due to the country's long history of welcoming immigrants and various pathways to legal immigration.

The idea that migration is a simple and straightforward process is a common misconception that glosses over the harsh realities and dangers experienced by individuals and families in pursuit of a better life. In recent years the US Immigration system has become more and more complex, bureaucratic, and creating long waiting times and significant financial costs. The journey to get to the US has also increased in difficulty.

For a very long time, the first things that Americans associated with migration were economic gain, financial aid and jumping the southern border. But the journey to the U.S. is by no means a stroll through Central Park followed by financial gains and prosperity for migrants. The trip from Central America to the U.S.A. is treacherous and filled with risks and dangers. Given that migrants experience dire conditions in their home town, they are ready to put all their savings and even their life on the line for an opportunity for migrating to the U.S. Most migrants never make it successfully to the U.S., because of the low number of legal migration means, the restrictive border access and the dangers encountered during the migration trip.

Myth #2 Remittances are a form of aid.

It is an unfortunate reality that in contemporary society, a common misconception persists that migration to the United States is a straightforward endeavor. Furthermore, migrants are often viewed through a negative lens, where they are scapegoated as the root cause of various economic and societal issues. Specifically, migrants are believed to be a drain on the American economy, absorbing resources without contributing to the nation's progress. However, this portrayal is fundamentally flawed and unjustified. It is crucial to examine the truth of the matter and shed light on the actual experiences of migrants in the US.

In a bid to force Mexico to fund the construction of the border wall, former President Trump threatened to cut off remittance payments from Mexican migrants in the US. This notion perpetuates the myth that remittances serve as a form of welfare for impoverished families in Central America, implying that these families rely solely on handouts without contributing to their own livelihoods.

A closer look at the World Food Program survey data, however, paints a different picture. It reveals that recipient families are actively working, with remittances constituting, on average, only x% of their household income. Furthermore, people should keep in mind that most families who receive remittances only allocate this money on basic needs to alleviate their financial constraints.

Additionally, it's important to highlight that while most recipient families may not be wealthy, they are not the poorest of the poor either. The high costs of international migration often prevent the poorest households from migrating and sending cross-border remittances. Consequently, remittances should not be seen as a substitute for government assistance, as they don't reach everyone, especially the most vulnerable populations.

Myth #3: Migrant workers have few vehicles to send money back; it takes a long time and is very expensive.

For years, the common perception has been that migrant workers struggle to find efficient and cost-effective methods to send money back to their families. However, our data-driven investigation into the remittance landscape tells a slightly different story.

Our analysis of the remittances price worldwide dataset from more than 1700 transactions whose destination region is in Latin America & Caribbeans from 2016 Q2 to 2022 Q3, reveals that the majority (90%) actually utilize money transfer service providers, such as Western Union, MoneyGram, for their remittance needs.

Data Scource: Remittances Price Worldwide (2016 Q2 - 2022 Q3)

Contrary to popular belief, these services offer competitive fees and exchange rates. Our data shows that the average cost of sending $200 through these providers is just 5.9% of the transaction amount, significantly lower than the 10-15% commonly cited. Furthermore, competition among providers has driven down fees in recent years, benefiting migrant workers and their families.

Money transfer services have also excelled in terms of speed and accessibility. Our findings indicate that 73.7% of transactions through these providers are completed within 24 hours, while 83% are processed within 48 hours. Additionally, these services boast an extensive global network, making it easy for recipients to access funds in rural and urban areas alike.

While these bank alternatives have helped in reducing the cost and time it takes to send money across borders. However, it is important to note that migrant workers still face challenges when it comes to sending remittances.

A significant challenge faced by migrant workers when sending remittances is the lack of readily available information to compare the costs and features of various service providers. With numerous options in the market, each offering different fees, exchange rates, and transaction speeds, it can be an overwhelming task for migrant workers to identify the most affordable and efficient service tailored to their specific needs. This information asymmetry can lead to suboptimal decisions, causing workers to use more expensive or slower services than necessary.
